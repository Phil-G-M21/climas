The story

We built CLIMAS because we had to.

Not because there was a market gap. Because there was a safety gap — and it was happening in our own homes.

It started with a phone call at 3 a.m. A family member had left a gas knob cracked open overnight. Everyone was fine — barely. That morning, CLIMAS began.

In Ghana, LPG is everywhere. Homes, office pantries, school kitchens, dorm common rooms, roadside food joints. Millions of cylinders, almost none of them monitored by anything smarter than a nose. That's the gap CLIMAS was built to fill: an affordable, always-on indoor safety device that doesn't ask you to download an app, read a manual, or trust your instincts when you're asleep.

The device itself is small and quiet. It sits in a corner , on a kitchen shelf, next to an office water dispenser, above a dormitory hallway door , and does one thing well: watch the air. Temperature, humidity, combustible gas. If readings go wrong, it reacts in seconds. A phone call to your emergency contact. A buzzer loud enough to wake a household. A red LED and an OLED screen that says one word: DANGER.

We built it on a capable ESP board with WiFi. We kept the internals deliberately simple ; fewer components means fewer points of failure, lower cost, and a device we can actually hand-assemble right here in Ghana, tested and shipped in five days. The firmware is non-blocking: even if your WiFi goes down, the gas sensor, the buzzer, and the LED keep working. Safety doesn't get to depend on an internet connection.

The hardware is free because it has to be. If the thing that stands between a family and a gas leak is a price tag, we've already failed. What you pay for is the service layer ; the alerting infrastructure, the live readings, the weather context, the support line when something goes wrong at 2 a.m. That's the subscription. ₵18 a month for the basics. ₵28 if you want the full online dashboard.

CLIMAS isn't just for homes. It's for offices with pantry kitchens. School science labs. College dormitories. Small restaurants. Server rooms where a gas heater runs in harmattan season. Anywhere there's a combustion source and people who'd rather know than guess.

We're a small team in Ghana, building something we believe every indoor space deserves. The device is the easy part , the hard part is making sure it reaches the people who need it. That's what we're working on now.
